# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Two audiences read the same pages and both must feel addressed:

- **Prospective donors.** Individuals and small foundations evaluating whether ASSP is a legitimate, well-governed 501(c)(3) worth backing. They arrive skeptical. They scan for board composition, program specifics, financial transparency, and signs that the organization is real rather than aspirational. They convert on credibility, not on emotion.
- **Athletes who want to learn more, and potentially submit a grant request.** Riders, skaters, mountain bikers, trail runners, and the trail crews and families around them. They land on the site to understand what ASSP does, find resources, and (for some) apply for grant support after an injury, gear gap, or near-miss. They are allergic to nonprofit-speak and to brands that perform "action sports" at them. They convert on usefulness and recognition.

The site exists in the seam between funder-facing institutional credibility and rider-facing practical utility. That seam is the brand.

## Product Purpose

The Action Sports Safety Project (ASSP) is a 501(c)(3) public-benefit nonprofit that funds safety education, gear access, and injury-recovery support inside action-sports communities. The site is the org's primary public surface.

**Primary outcome:** drive donations. Every page is downstream of that goal.

**Supporting outcomes that make donations possible:**

1. Establish institutional legitimacy for prospective donors (board, programs, governance, financials).
2. Surface programs so athletes can learn what ASSP does and access it.
3. Capture grant requests from athletes and partner organizations.

Success looks like a first-time donor who arrived from a search result, scrolled, read enough specifics to trust the org, and gave. In the same week, a rider lands on the same homepage, finds the grants page, and submits a real application.

## Brand Personality

**Three words:** grounded, honest, warm.

**Voice:** plainspoken, specific, never performative. The org speaks the way a thoughtful program director would talk in a parking lot after a long ride: direct, no sales gloss, no jargon, no crisis theater. Confident enough not to shout. **The voice is a knowledgeable friend who rides, not a press release.**

**Emotional goals:** trust, recognition, quiet resolve. A donor should feel they are funding adults doing real work. A rider should feel the org is by people who actually ride, not by people who studied action sports for a deck.

**Creative concept, Terrain & Community.** The visual and verbal language pulls from the physical landscapes action-sports communities live in. Trail dust. Pine forests. The warmth of a parking lot debrief after a long ride. Weathered, not vintage. Earned, not nostalgic.

### Voice & Tone

**Use:**

- **People words:** community, riders, trail crews, athletes.
- **Verbs of action:** prepare, equip, train.
- **Inclusive framing:** "everyone deserves".
- **Concrete proof:** real impact, real people.
- **Named disciplines:** skating, mountain biking, trail running. Name the sport, never "action sports" as a vague catch-all when a specific discipline fits.

**Avoid:**

- **Action-sports cliché:** extreme, hardcore.
- **Corporate-speak:** synergy, leverage, scalable.
- **Othering:** "the underserved", "at-risk youth", any framing that turns participants into a category.
- **Overused inspiration words:** life-changing, transformative, empower.
- **Tech-bro tropes:** cutting-edge, next-generation, disruptive.
- **Nonprofit-industrial-complex jargon:** stakeholders, capacity-building, impact-driven, mission-aligned.

If a sentence could appear unchanged in a press release, rewrite it.

## Anti-references

This site must not resemble any of the following. Every one is a default the category falls into; ASSP is in the seam between them and reads as none of them.

- **Nonprofit-template charity site.** Stock photos of smiling kids, soft blue and green gradients, "Make a difference today" hero, sidebar donation thermometer, badge soup in the footer.
- **Safety-bureaucracy or government.** Cold institutional blues, compliance gray, dense PDF aesthetics, .gov-shaped header strips.
- **Action-sports or energy-drink brand.** Hard black, neon yellow or red, motocross slashes, aggressive italic caps, "extreme" anything.
- **SaaS startup.** Purple-to-pink gradients, abstract hero illustrations, pricing cards, hero-metric grids, glassmorphism.
- **GoFundMe or charity-water emotional appeal.** Single full-bleed photo, sans-serif tagline overlay, big yellow CTA, beneficiary-as-spectacle framing.

If a draft could be mistaken for any of the above, restart the section.

## Design Principles

1. **Earned, not performed.** Legitimacy comes from specifics: named programs, named board members, real outcomes, real financials. Never from stock imagery, awareness ribbons, or feel-good copy.
2. **Honest materials.** The visual language reads as terrain: dust, pine, weathered wood, warm paper. No glossy gradients, no fake textures, no chrome, no glass. If a surface needs decoration to justify itself, the surface is wrong.
3. **Two audiences, one voice.** Funders and riders read the same page and both feel addressed. Never code-switch between an institutional voice and an action-sports voice between sections. The org is one thing; the site sounds like one thing.
4. **Donate is the spine.** Pine carries the page. Clay punctuates it. Every page leads somewhere that supports the primary ask, but Clay is a tertiary color, not a carry color: it appears at most twice on any page, only on the highest-priority CTA (the donate or apply-for-grant action), and never as decoration. No Clay tag bullets, no Clay monograms, no Clay-drenched sections. If Clay is on a page in more than two places, two of those places are wrong.
5. **Quiet authority over urgency.** Persuasion comes from typographic hierarchy, generous space, and committed color, not from countdown timers, crisis framing, or shouted CTAs. The site is calm because the work is serious.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

- **Target:** WCAG 2.2 AA across the site, including color contrast, focus visibility, keyboard navigation, and semantic landmarks.
- **Reduced motion:** all non-essential motion respects `prefers-reduced-motion: reduce`. Scroll-driven and parallax effects degrade to static.
- **Reading conditions to design for:** older donors on desktop in bright rooms; athletes reading on phones outdoors with glare; readers who may be recovering from concussion or TBI and should not be overwhelmed by motion, flashing, or dense walls of small type.
- **Color independence:** Clay-as-action must always be paired with a second signal (label, underline, position). Never rely on color alone to communicate state or interactivity.
- **Forms (donate, grant application, contact):** labels visible, errors specific and inline, no time-outs, no captchas that block screen readers.
